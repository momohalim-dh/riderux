The question underneath

What relies purely on human judgment?

Execution is free. AI can already generate layouts, flows, copy variants, research transcripts, and synthesis drafts faster and cheaper than any team. The question is not whether that changes our work. It does. The question is what is left that only we can do.

Below is a first map of the UX moat: the areas where human judgment is not just useful but irreplaceable.

Inspired by Pandora's manifesto, some content adjusted to suit Rider UX context and status quo.

Empathy & storytelling
To translate what we know about riders into something a team can build toward and believe in requires empathy, judgment and intent. That translation from insight to direction to shipped experience, is the craft.
Systems thinking
Accelerate design system: use AI to automate component development, unblock the backlog, and empower designers and engineers to review and ship output rather than build from scratch.
Conceptual discovery
Finding new interaction paradigms, not refining existing ones. AI generates variations inside a pattern. Shifting the pattern requires human imagination and taste.
Synthesis at scale
Cross-study pattern identification, connecting qualitative and quantitative signals, building thematic repositories from years of research. AI can surface patterns. UX decides which ones matter and what they mean for the product.
Strategic framing
Knowing what is worth building. Not just "what do riders want" but "what should we build and why does it matter." Aligning what we build with values, context, and user history. That framing is ours.
Simulation ownership
Running designs through AI-generated user models to test concepts before real research. The UX lens on what to simulate, how to interpret results, and what it means for the rider is ours, wherever ownership formally lands.
How each role evolves

A proposal for what the roles become

When the doing is no longer the definition, what is? Below is a first attempt at reframing each role around what it owns rather than what it produces.

01
UX Designer
+
The designer's role shifts from producing screens to owning the end-to-end experience logic: the decisions about what should exist, how it connects, and why it matters to the business and the rider. Their real value is pattern recognition across contexts that no single squad can see.
AI takes over
  • Layout and flow generation
  • Component specs and annotation
  • Prototype variants
  • Accessibility checks
  • Design token maintenance
Designer owns
  • Taste: knowing when something is wrong before you can say why
  • Experience coherence across surfaces and releases
  • Judgment on when to break the pattern
  • Emotional resonance decisions
  • Accountability for outcomes, not artifacts
02
UX Researcher
+
Research stops being a service that produces reports on request and becomes the intelligence layer of the product org. The researcher becomes the team's early warning system, proactively surfacing tensions, assumptions, and blind spots before they become expensive. AI handles the data pipeline; the researcher handles meaning and stakes.
AI takes over
  • Transcription and tagging
  • Synthesis first drafts
  • Recruitment and scheduling
  • Survey analysis
  • Competitive benchmarking
Researcher owns
  • Deciding which insight changes a decision vs. which is noise
  • Framing the right question before anyone asks
  • Ethical judgment in data collection and interpretation
  • Sense-making across contradictory signals
  • Advocating for the rider when data could be misread
03
UX Writer
+
When AI can generate a hundred copy variants in seconds, the writer's value is no longer in producing words. It is in defining the rules, voice, and boundaries that make AI-generated language trustworthy, on-brand, and locally relevant. The role evolves from a content service into a content system: the person who designs how language works in the product, not just what it says.
AI takes over
  • Copy variants and first drafts
  • Localization prep
  • Consistency auditing
  • Error message cataloguing
  • Push notification drafts
Writer owns
  • Tonal judgment in high-stakes moments: errors, cancellations, safety
  • Voice decisions that cannot be templated
  • Cultural sensitivity in new markets
  • Knowing when AI copy is correct but feels wrong
  • The line between clarity and coldness
How roles might develop

Two orientations, nothing decided yet

As UX moves upstream toward judgment and strategy, two natural directions are emerging within the discipline. These are not formal tracks. They are a starting frame for a conversation we will need to have with the wider team. Both orientations can connect into a third role: UX as the bridge that holds user understanding across PM, engineering, ops, and business: a function no other discipline is structured to own.

Orientation A
Closer to the user
  • Strategic framing and problem definition
  • Research synthesis and insight direction
  • Prompt prototyping and concept evaluation
  • Human-centered evaluation of AI outputs
  • Synthetic user simulation and interpretation
  • Connecting user understanding to ops and business
Orientation B
Closer to the code
  • Design system evolution and AI integration
  • Agentic prototyping and spec generation
  • Design QA automation
  • Tool building and team enablement
  • AI governance for language and components
  • Code contributions alongside engineering

Both orientations support the connector role. Whether someone leans closer to the user or closer to the code, the opportunity is the same: UX as the function that synthesizes across disciplines and holds the experience together at a level no one else is positioned to own.

This is not just a UX conversation

AI transformation requires the whole org to move together

Each function has its own evolution roadmap to define. But making the transformation happen requires the org to move as one. A few things that need to be true across functions, not just within UX:

  • Redefine how we work together as an org: how we structure ourselves, what foundations we prioritize, and how we commit to each other's momentum, not just our own.
  • UX and engineering need to align on how to catch up and scale the design system together: which initiatives to prioritize, how to measure success, how to iterate, and what comes next.
  • Some of this will happen in parallel. But it needs shared attention and commitment to accelerate what matters to us as an org, not just as separate parts.
  • AI investment across DH needs to translate into measurable outcomes, not just activity. How we define and track UX impact when the execution layer is no longer the primary measure is a question we need to answer together.
Open questions

What we want to align on at the org level

This document is a seed. A few questions that need to be answered at the leadership level before Rider UX Team can produce something actionable:

  1. What are the AI priorities at the Rider PL level that UX is expected to contribute to? What does DH expect from the UX function in the AI agenda?
  2. As PM and engineering become more independently capable with AI tools, how do we redefine what a cross-functional team looks like? Who owns what, and when is UX in the room?
  3. If UX moves upstream toward judgment and strategy, who is accountable for the quality of what AI produces? How do we make sure that is explicit and not assumed?
  4. How do we measure UX impact when the execution layer is no longer the primary signal? What does success look like in a token economy where outcomes matter more than output?
  5. What is the direction on how we measure someone's performance when AI is integrated into day-to-day work and collaboration? What do we want to protect and what needs to change?
  6. What foundations need product and engineering commitment to actually move? What are we asking of each other, and how do we hold each other to it?

This is a first draft on a topic that will take the rest of the year to define properly, and even continue iterating it beyond. The goal is not to have all the answers now. It is to walk out of the next leadership conversation with enough shared direction across Design, Product, Engineering.
